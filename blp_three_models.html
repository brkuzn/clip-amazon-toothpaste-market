CLIP Nesting The Thesis Contribution
What is CLIP? CLIP (Contrastive LanguageImage Pre-training, Radford et al. 2021) is a neural network trained to jointly embed product images and text descriptions into the same vector space. Products that look similar AND read similarly end up nearby in CLIP space. We use this geometry to define demand substitution nests replacing the ad-hoc assumption that brand identity determines consumer switching patterns.

Construction of CLIP Nests

  1. For each ASIN: product image + listing text CLIP 512-dim embedding $\mathbf{e}_j$
  2. PCA across all 163 panel ASINs retain 5 principal components (joint_pc15)
  3. K-means, $K=5$, seed=42, 50 restarts cluster assignments $g(j)\in\{1,\ldots,6\}$
  4. 71.2% of PC15 variance explained by centroids
  5. These cluster IDs become the nest column in Model 3
Why this matters economically: Brand-based nesting says "all Colgate products substitute among themselves first." CLIP nesting says "products that look and read alike substitute more, regardless of brand." The two framings imply different diversion ratios and different merger price effects.
hello vs Colgate in CLIP space: hello products cluster with natural/specialty brands (Cluster 5: APAGARD, JASON, Other). Colgate occupies its own cluster (Cluster 2). They are in different nests, so their cross-price substitution uses the weaker different-nest formula. This is why M3 predicts a smaller merger price effect than M1 or M2.

Formula

$$\underbrace{\log\frac{s_{jt}}{s_{0t}}}_{\text{log-odds}} - \rho\,\underbrace{\log s_{j|g,t}}_{\text{within-nest}} = \alpha\,p_{jt} + \boldsymbol{\beta}'\mathbf{x}_{jt} + \text{FE}_t + \xi_{jt}$$

  • $\rho\in[0,1)$ nesting intensity; = 0 in M1 (term drops), grid-selected in M2/M3
  • $\alpha$ price sensitivity, calibrated identically across all models
  • $\text{FE}_t$ quarter fixed effects, absorbed by within-quarter demeaning

All three studies use differentiated-product discrete-choice demand on CPG scanner data the closest analog to our Amazon ASIN-level toothpaste panel. The consensus range is 1.5 to 3.0; our target of 2.62 (Bijmolt et al. 2005 mean) is well within this range.

OLS is biased upward because popular products charge higher prices (simultaneity bias). Due to the well-documented challenges of finding valid cost-shifters in high-frequency online retail data, the category price elasticity is calibrated to the CPG brand-level meta-analysis mean (2.62, Bijmolt et al. 2005). The primary contribution of this thesis is demonstrating how CLIP embeddings (PC1PC5) successfully map the complex product differentiation space, enabling precise attribute-based diversion ratios in the merger simulation.

Sequential calibration implied elasticity at *: is calibrated so that the logit (=0) median own-price elasticity equals 2.62. Once ρ* is estimated (0.35 for M2, 0.67 for M3), the nested-logit elasticity formula $\varepsilon_{jj}=\alpha p_j[1/(1-\rho)-\rho s_{j|g}/(1-\rho)-s_j]$ amplifies by roughly $1/(1-\rho^*)$ relative to the logit case. At ρ*=0.67 this factor is approximately 3.0, so M3's actual median own-price elasticity is approximately −7 to −8 — larger in magnitude than the calibration target. This is a known feature of the sequential (not joint) calibration procedure and does not invalidate the merger simulation; the merger effect ( ) is internally consistent because both Nash simulations use the same (, *). The thesis should report the implied elasticity at each * alongside the calibration target.

Formula

For each on a fine grid, the optimal is computed analytically (one OLS). Pick * = argmin RSS:

$$\tilde{y}_{jt}(\rho) = \log\tfrac{s_{jt}}{s_{0t}} - \alpha p_{jt} - \rho\log s_{j|g,t}$$ $$\hat\beta(\rho)=(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\tilde{y}(\rho)$$ $$\rho^* = \arg\min_{\rho\in[0,0.90]}\|\tilde{y}(\rho)-\mathbf{X}\hat\beta(\rho)\|^2$$

Demand Jacobian = s/p Where Models Diverge

Three cases

Own (k=j), all models:

$$\Delta_{jj}=\alpha s_j\!\left[\tfrac{1}{1-\rho}-\tfrac{\rho}{1-\rho}s_{j|g}-s_j\right]<0$$

Cross, same nest, >0:

$$\Delta_{kj}=\alpha s_k\!\left[-\tfrac{\rho}{1-\rho}s_{j|g}-s_j\right]>0$$

Cross, different nest (or =0):

$$\Delta_{kj}=-\alpha s_k s_j>0\;(\alpha<0)$$
M1: =0

All cross: $-\alpha s_k s_j$. No amplification.

M2: =0.35

Same-brand amplified by $\rho/(1{-}\rho)\approx0.54$. helloColgate = diff nest.

M3: ρ=0.67

Same-cluster amplified by $\rho/(1{-}\rho)\approx2.03$. hello & Colgate in different clusters = diff nest.

Delta <- matrix(0, J, J)
for (j in 1:J) for (k in 1:J) {
  sn <- (nest_vec[j] == nest_vec[k])
  if (j == k) {
    Delta[j,j] <- alpha * s[j] *
      (1/(1-rho) - rho/(1-rho)*sjg[j] - s[j])
  } else if (sn && rho > 0) {
    Delta[j,k] <- alpha * s[j] *
      (-rho/(1-rho)*sjg[k] - s[k])
  } else {
    Delta[j,k] <- -alpha * s[j] * s[k]
  }
}

Bertrand MC Inversion

Formula

$$\mathbf{mc} = \mathbf{p} + (\Omega_{\text{pre}}\circ\Delta)^{-1}\mathbf{s}$$

At the observed pre-merger equilibrium each firm satisfies its FOC. Inverting recovers MCs that rationalize observed prices under pre-merger ownership. Since $(\Omega\circ\Delta)^{-1}\mathbf{s}$ is a negative vector (own-effects dominate), $mc < p$.

Negative MCs why they occur and how we handle them:
  • Cause: Products with very small market shares produce near-singular rows in (). The inversion amplifies noise, occasionally yielding mc < 0. This is a demand-side misspecification signal.
  • Rate: M1/M2: 8.6% negative M3 (CLIP-nested): 1.7% negative. The threefold reduction confirms that CLIP clusters capture the substitution structure better than brand nests.
  • Treatment: No floor is applied the Nash contraction runs on raw inverted MCs, including the negative values. In practice this is harmless: all negative MCs belong to low-share fringe products (Orajel, sparse "Other" ASINs), none of which are merger parties. Their shares are too small to distort the price updates for hello or Colgate, and the contraction converges normally. As a robustness check, results with a 30%-of-price floor differ by less than 0.08 percentage points in the merger effect confirming the negative MCs are economically inconsequential for the simulation.
  • Interpretation: CLIP nesting yields substantially more plausible MCs — the reduction from 8.6% to 1.7% (K=5) is strong evidence that the embedding-based nesting structure is the appropriate demand specification for this market.

Merger Simulation Methodology, Counterfactual & Convergence
The merger actually happened (2020Q1). Colgate acquired hello and the observed prices from 2020Q1 onwards were already set by the merged firm. We cannot observe the world where the merger did not happen that world must be simulated. We run two Nash fixed-points and compare them; observed prices are shown for context but are not part of the effect calculation.

The three price series what each one is:

Observed price
The actual transaction price from the Amazon data. Set by the real merged firm (from 2020Q1 onwards), subject to all real-world forces: cost shocks, demand trends, competitor behaviour, Amazon algorithms, promotions. Not used in the effect calculation shown only as a sanity check on model fit. The gap between observed and Nash Merger reflects everything our structural model does not capture.
Nash No-merger (counterfactual)
The Nash equilibrium prices our model predicts if hello had remained a separate firm. Uses ownership matrix $\Omega_{\text{pre}}$ hello maximises its own profit independently, ignoring any revenue Colgate earns from customers who leave hello. This world never existed in the data after 2020Q1; it is a pure model construct. This is the counterfactual baseline.
Nash Merger (model-predicted factual)
The Nash equilibrium prices our model predicts under merged ownership. Uses $\Omega_{\text{post}}$ hello's ASINs are reassigned to Colgate's firm ID, so the merged firm jointly optimises over both brands. This is the structural model's ceteris paribus prediction of post-merger pricing. It should be close to observed prices in principle, but will not match exactly because our model is a simplification.

The only difference between and is the ownership matrix

Everything else is held identical: the demand parameters (, , ), the product-level marginal costs inverted from the pre-merger FOC, the mean utilities , and the full product set. The two simulations are run on the same data with the same model only the ownership indicator changes.

No-merger: _pre
firm_id_pre[hello ASINs] = hello_fid
hello's FOC: maximise $(p_h - mc_h)\cdot s_h$ only.
Customers leaving hello are lost revenue hello has no stake in where they go.
Merger: _post
firm_id_post[hello ASINs] = colgate_fid
Merged FOC: maximise $(p_h - mc_h)\cdot s_h + (p_c - mc_c)\cdot s_c$.
Customers leaving hello who switch to Colgate are retained revenue the merged firm internalises this.

Why does the merger raise prices? the internalization mechanism

When hello raises its price by a small amount $\delta p$, it loses some customers: $\partial s_{\text{hello}}/\partial p_{\text{hello}} < 0$. A fraction of those customers switch to Colgate products: $\partial s_{\text{colgate}}/\partial p_{\text{hello}} > 0$.

Stand-alone hello FOC:
$$\frac{\partial \pi_h}{\partial p_h} = s_h + (p_h - mc_h)\frac{\partial s_h}{\partial p_h} = 0$$ The term $\partial s_c / \partial p_h$ does not appear hello ignores the Colgate revenue it creates.
Merged firm FOC for hello's price:
$$\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial p_h} = s_h + (p_h - mc_h)\frac{\partial s_h}{\partial p_h} + (p_c - mc_c)\frac{\partial s_c}{\partial p_h} = 0$$ The extra term $(p_c - mc_c)\,\partial s_c/\partial p_h > 0$ makes the FOC satisfied at a higher $p_h$.

The size of the price increase depends on the diversion ratio $DR_{h\to c} = -(\partial s_c/\partial p_h)/(\partial s_h/\partial p_h)$ the fraction of hello defectors who go to Colgate. Higher diversion larger merger effect. This is exactly where CLIP nesting matters: because hello (Cluster 5) and Colgate (Cluster 2) are in different embedding clusters, M3 produces a lower diversion ratio and therefore a smaller predicted price increase (+0.40%) than M1 (+1.23%) and M2 (+1.28%).

Why doesn't Nash Merger match observed prices exactly?

In theory, if our model were perfect, Nash Merger should reproduce the observed post-merger prices (since both are the outcome of merged-firm optimisation). In practice they differ because:
  • Ceteris paribus: Our simulation holds everything fixed except the ownership switch no cost changes over time, no new entry, no macroeconomic shocks, no Amazon promotional effects.
  • Calibrated : We use a single price coefficient for all products. Real consumers differ in price sensitivity across products and over time.
  • No unobserved cost shocks: The structural error jt absorbs cost and demand shocks that shift actual prices but are not in our model.
  • Model is identified on pre-merger data: Marginal costs are inverted from the pre-merger FOC and held fixed. Real MCs change with input costs, logistics, and scale.
The gap between Nash Merger and observed is not an error in the merger effect it is a model fit issue. The merger effect ( ) is model-consistent by construction and does not depend on how well the model fits the absolute price level.

Bertrand-Nash FOC & Fixed-Point

1. Equilibrium condition:

$$\mathbf{p}^* = \mathbf{mc} - \bigl(\Omega\circ\Delta(\mathbf{p}^*)\bigr)^{-1}\mathbf{s}(\mathbf{p}^*)$$

Both $\Delta$ and $\mathbf{s}$ depend on current prices through mean utility $V_j = (\delta_j^{\text{pre}} - \alpha p_j^{\text{pre}}) + \alpha p_j$. The non-price component is held fixed product characteristics don't change, only prices move.

2. Damped iteration:

$$\mathbf{p}_{(n+1)} = \lambda\underbrace{\!\left[\mathbf{mc} - (\Omega\circ\Delta(\mathbf{p}_{(n)}))^{-1}\mathbf{s}(\mathbf{p}_{(n)})\right]\!}_{\text{Nash step}} + (1-\lambda)\,\mathbf{p}_{(n)}$$

Stops when $\max_j|p_{(n+1)}-p_{(n)}|<10^{-8}$. Max 2,000 iterations.

3. Ownership matrices:

$\Omega[j,k]=\mathbf{1}[\text{firm}_j=\text{firm}_k]$
Post-merger: firm$^{\text{post}}_j=\text{colgate\_fid}$ for $j\in\text{hello}$, quarter$\geq$2020Q1

# Generic Nash for any ownership structure
run_nash <- function(firm_col) {
  rbindlist(lapply(merger_qtrs, function(qtr) {
    dt_q      <- pan[quarter==qtr][order(asin)]
    firms_use <- dt_q[[firm_col]]  #  key
    mc_q      <- dt_q$mc_fixed    # pre-merger avg MC, frozen
    p <- nash_bertrand(
      delta_pre=dt_q$delta, p_pre=dt_q$price_mean,
      mc=mc_q, firms_post=firms_use,
      alpha=ALPHA, rho=rho_star, nests=nv)
    data.table(asin=dt_q$asin, quarter=qtr, p=p)
  }))
}

Why R instead of PyBLP here? PyBLP's compute_prices() uses the identical Morrow & Skerlos (2011) contraction the algorithm is the same. However, PyBLP's outer GMM loop identifies via moment conditions that require valid price instruments (supply-side cost-shifters). Our data lack such instruments, so PyBLP's hits the 0.90 boundary. Our R concentrated OLS profile (grid search over , argmin RSS) is more appropriate given the calibration approach, and delivers the same Nash contraction once and are fixed. The two implementations produce identical results for any given (, , mc, ).

📊 Appendix A: K Sensitivity Analysis — Selecting the Number of CLIP Clusters

The number of k-means clusters K is a modelling choice. We select it by jointly optimising four criteria evaluated across K = 2–10:

  1. Cluster quality — average silhouette width (higher = better separation in CLIP space; peaks near K=5)
  2. Demand fit — RSS drop vs ρ=0 from the concentrated OLS profile (higher = stronger within-nest substitutability signal)
  3. Economic plausibility — share of negative marginal costs (lower = more credible cost estimates)
  4. Merger-effect stability — hello and Colgate must land in different clusters for the diversion ratio to be economically meaningful

K=2–4 produce implausibly large hello merger effects (+1.7–4.0%) because the coarse nesting collapses hello and Colgate into the same broad cluster, inflating the implied diversion ratio. K≥7 shows declining RSS improvement and rising negative-MC rates as nests fragment. K=5 maximises the silhouette (0.388), achieves the highest RSS drop (8.1%), yields the lowest negative-MC rate (1.7%), and keeps the merger effect economically small (+0.40% for hello) — making it the dominant choice across all four criteria. K=6 is the closest competitor (silhouette 0.387, RSS drop 6.7%, neg-MC 3.4%) but is strictly dominated by K=5 on every metric.
